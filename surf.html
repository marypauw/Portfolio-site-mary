Surf

The clickable points feature

Surf goal is to empower people with control over their data. people with control over their data. Users can download the Surf extension and be rewarded in exchange for their browsing data. Surf shares browsing, demographic and behavioral data with companies that might be interested in these kinds of data but in an anonymous way.

At Surf I am responsible for the UX and UI Design of 3 different products (one B2B and the other B2C) but in this project, I would like to showcase a specific feature that was designed and developer after two usability testing sessions.

This is a pretty rare case of a feature that has been tested twice, it would normally not happen in our testing process but I had the opportunity to validate my designs twice and this really made the new feature very user friendly.

Prototipying and Testing

 
01

The problem

The Surf and its extension have been launched in September 2021. The users have to sign up for Surf and download the browser extension to start earning points in exchange for their browsing data. The users can pause the extension or delete browsing data from their activity history.

In January 2022 we decided to perform the first usability testing session for Surf to understand how usable and valuable the first iteration of the Surf product is.

  • What room for improvement do we have, that we don't know of yet?
  • What issues will a user encounter when trying to use Surf?

 
02

Testing

The usability testing has been performed with 6 participants as a moderated testing through Zoom call. We prepared the test plan in Dovetail, a software that we use to write down the test script, take notes, tag and organize findings and insights to share with the product team. The participants were asked first to first reply to a few questions about themselves, mostly demographics questions and a few about their experience with GPT (Get-Paid-to) apps or websites.

Later, users were asked to perform a few tasks: create a Surf account, go through the onboarding process, start surfing to gather some data, try to delete their browsing history, and save an item in their wish list. Participants were asked to think out loud and share their feedback with us during the testing session.

During the testing session, we noticed that a few participants tried to click on the points on the right side of the navigation even if that element was not clickable.

 
03

Insights

The user testing sessions were very productive and we could write down several findings that we could share with the product manager and the developers. A few ideas for new features and improvements came out of this testing. For this case we will take into consideration just a few:

  • Some users would expect the points in the navigation to be clickable
  • The page displaying their browsing history (called activity history) is not always easy to find
  • Users were asking if deleting items from their browsing history would make them lose points
  • Deleting activity history was confused with the Pause feature in the extension
  • Participants understood how to earn points but would have liked to have more insights into how to accumulate more

The first finding was also confirmed by analyzing the click heatmap provided by the product manager through Hotjar The map was displaying that a lot of people tried to click the points in the navigation.

 

Ideation and design

 
04

Low fidelity design

After discussing the insights with the product manager and discussing possible solutions for our new problems with the developers, I created a first low-fidelity draft of the design in Whimsical. We decided to add several features and changes to solve some of the points of friction with the user:

  • Make the points in the navigation clickable and a dropdown section would appear on click
  • From the dropdown section, the user would be able to navigate to the activity history page making the navigation to this page easier
  • Add an info text about how to earn points and how deleting the browsing history affect their points
  • Add a breakdown of the user points and how they were earned by the user

The biggest challenge for me was trying to accommodate both the user's and the business's needs.

User needs
understand how they have earned their points and how to earn more.

Business needs
Surf should not influence the browsing behavior of the user. We don't want the user to change their browsing habits to earn more points but to gather genuine data.

My proposal
try to show more information to the user about how they earned bonus points or how many points they used redeeming points and add a weekly and monthly breakdown of the points without displaying the exact number of points earned.

05

High fidelity design

After discussing the low-fidelity draft with the project manager and the developers I could start designing a high fidelity version of this feature in Figma, which we started calling "clickable points" but in reality was including way more than just making the points in the navigation clickable.

In the meantime, we were preparing to launch a new feature: discount codes, and offers in Surf. We decided to launch another session of usability testing to understand what our users would expect from this new upcoming feature. I decided to use this opportunity to test also the new designs of the "clickable points" feature and I created an interactive prototype in Figma to be able to test this during our moderated sessions.

Testing and validating

 
06

Usability testing

In the second usability testing session, the users were presented with an interactive prototype of the feature of the "clickable point". Some of the questions asked to the users were:

  • How do you expect to get more points?
  • Where would you look for a breakdown or summary of your points
  • Do you understand how your points were earned?

Most of the users were very happy with the new feature and instinctively clicked on the points in the navigation to know more about how their points were earned. But another point of friction was discovered during the testing session.

Some users had some confusion around weekly and current points when opening the dropdown of the clickable points.

But most of the users were satisfied with the transparency of the points which meant that the current solution was covering both user and business needs.

see final prototype
 
07

The final design

Thanks to the second testing we understood that the dropdown appearing when clicking on the points in the navigation was unnecessary and confusing. So together with the product manager, we decided to get rid of this step and the user would have landed directly on the activity history page (today called my data page) when clicking on the points in the navigation. This made the navigation to this page quicker and avoid us from brainstorming around the confusing verbiage that was presented in the dropdown in the first design draft.

Main problems highlighted during the usability testing:

  • h4 and text input fields are too small- Need to be increased from 12px to 14px
  • The double menu on the left and right is confusing. Users would search for the profile information in the hamburger menu
  • Layout of the property page needs to be improved
  • Some users would prefer to set their alerts from the filter selection instead in their profile

The only point that I decided to not change for the moment is the second because I think that users would be confused only the first time they use the product but then remember the navigation pattern when they will come back. This point will need further testing in the future.

conclusion

The new feature of the clickable points has been taken as a great example of why we perform user research and usability testing before jumping into development.

This project was showcased during our weekly session of Product Q&A for the other departments (sales, marketing, and customer success) and I was asked to make a quick video representation to explain why we decided to introduce these changes and how usability testing helped us to make the right design decisions to create a user-friendly product.

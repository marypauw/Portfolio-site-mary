DRAC

DRAC is the first project I realised during my UX studies and it is a vocabulary app that wants to empower people to learn new vocabularies of languages, other than your mother tongue. DRAC is a mobile app thought for people of any age range, who are interested in learning a new language.

The words will be organized in cards where users can discover the definition of the word, sentences related to them and write down their notes. From the words of the day, users will be able to save words in their vocabulary or to play a game related to a specific contest.

Discover

 
01

Understanding the problem

The goal of the project was to create a vocabulary application that could suite to people of any age range, which want to broaden their vocabulary. The first step towards understanding the problem for me was to conduct some competitive analysis, to see how other designers solved my similar problem.

 
02

Interviews and surveys

To hear directly what the user needs from potential user I launched an online survey and interviewed in person three different people with different backgrounds, interests and needs. This helped me to receive more precious informations about how to solve the problem. and what users expect from a vocabulary application.

 
03

Empathy maps and user persona

After gathering all the information, I organized them in emphaty maps for every participant that I interviewed. Then, based on these information, I created one main proto-persona: Jack Gagliardi. Jack has passions and he is willing to expand his vocabulary and learn a new language, but most of all Jack as a precise need. At this point, I could start designing a vocabulary application for Jack.

 

Ideate

 
04

Concepting

Before approaching the design in itself, I created some task analysis and user flows in order to build up a basic architecture for the application. Moreover, I conducted a card sorting test in order to step back from my mental models.

 
05

Prototyping

At this point of the project I started creating some wireframes which I later developed in paper low-fidelity designs with the Crazy 8s time blocking method, roughly sketching eight different solutions to the same design problem in eight minutes. At the end I created the first low-fidelity clickable prototype with Prott.

 

Test

 
06

Usability Testing

At this stage of the project, the prototype was ready for a usability testing. I selected some participants and tested the low-fidelity prototype. I examined my results basing them on the Jakob Nielsen’s Five Components of Usability, which covers learnability, efficiency, memorability, satisfaction, and errors and wrote a usability test report.

 
07

The solution

Thanks to the feedback received during the usability testing phase I could create a new prototype. The testing helped me to step back from my designs, not getting attached to them and watch the whole project with a more critical eye. The main issue was related to the top navigation: initially, I created a homepage with no functions, a section where creating the card and a section where to review them. The participants were very confused also by the icons that I draw. The second prototype presented clearer navigation since the user can now already have access to the words of the day from the homepage, review cards in the vocabulary and play games in a separate section.

what's next

The project DRAC had been developed only as a low fidelity prototype so it still has the potentials to go through further stages of design and, thanks to more usability tests, to be bettered visually and concerning the experience.

I think that when developing the mid- and high- fidelity prototypes the following functionalities can be improved:

  • Organisation of the information in the card
  • The game/s
  • The categories and the possibility to create new one
